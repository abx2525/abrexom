Saffron

Why Herat Valley produces
the world's finest saffron

There is a reason Afghan saffron commands the highest prices on the international market. That reason is geography — and the centuries of farming knowledge that has evolved within it.

The altitude advantage

Herat Valley sits at approximately 900 metres above sea level in western Afghanistan. The elevation creates a climate of extremes: long, hot, dry summers that stress the saffron crocus into producing a higher concentration of aromatic compounds, followed by cold winters that allow the corms to rest and strengthen before the next season's bloom.

The result is a saffron stigma with a measurably higher concentration of crocin (the compound responsible for colour), safranal (fragrance), and picrocrocin (bitterness) than saffron grown at lower altitudes or in more temperate climates. Afghan saffron grown under these conditions is consistently regarded by chefs and buyers as among the finest in the world.

The harvest window

Saffron crocus (Crocus sativus) blooms for just two to three weeks each October. The flowers open at dawn and must be picked within hours, before the heat of the day wilts the delicate petals. Each flower contains exactly three stigmas — the red threads we prize — and they must be separated by hand. There is no mechanisation that does this without destroying what you're trying to collect.

In the Herat Valley, families rise before sunrise during harvest season and work through the morning in the fields. A skilled picker might harvest 150 to 200 grams of dried saffron in an entire season. This is why saffron is expensive: it is simply one of the most labour-intensive crops in the world.

The Abrexom difference

Most saffron sold globally is blended — stigmas from different regions and different quality grades mixed together to hit a price point. We do not do this. Every Abrexom jar contains saffron from the Herat Valley, sourced directly from local farming families. Afghanistan's saffron sector is not industrialised. There are no large processing facilities or certification bodies — just farmers who have grown this flower for centuries, and who do it better than anyone else in the world.

History

The ancient routes that carried
saffron to the world

The Silk Road was not a single road. It was a network of trade routes spanning more than 10,000 kilometres, connecting the civilisations of China, India, Central Asia, Persia, Arabia, and the Mediterranean over more than fifteen centuries. And among the most valuable commodities carried along those routes was saffron.

From Herat to the world

The Herat Valley occupied one of the most strategically important positions in the ancient world — at the junction of routes flowing north to Samarkand and Bukhara, east to Kashgar and ultimately to Chang'an, south to Kandahar and India, and west to Persia and Mesopotamia. Any merchant who wanted to move goods between the great civilisations passed through or near Herat.

Saffron was a natural export. The climate of the Herat Valley was (and remains) ideal for Crocus sativus. By the first millennium BCE, Afghan saffron was well established as a luxury item traded across the known world. Persian kings used it to dye their robes. Greek physicians prescribed it for melancholy and digestive ailments. Roman emperors scattered it in their bathwater and on the streets during processions.

What the merchants carried

The popular image of the Silk Road is dominated by silk — Chinese silk heading west in exchange for gold, glass, and horses. But silk was never the only commodity. Merchants carried pepper, cinnamon, and cardamom from India; lapis lazuli from the mines of Badakhshan; paper and printing techniques from China; glasswork from Rome and Alexandria; and from Afghanistan's highlands, saffron.

The Silk Road was not simply a trade route. It was the mechanism by which human knowledge, art, religion, and culture spread across Eurasia. Buddhism traveled the Silk Road from India to China. Islam spread along it from Arabia to Southeast Asia. The bubonic plague, too, followed its routes. Every commodity carried cultural meaning alongside its material value.

The name we chose

The Persian word for the Silk Road is raah abreshom — literally, the silk road. Abreshom means silk. When we founded Abrexom, we took that word and made it new: not a preservation of the past, but a revival of its spirit — the idea that rare and precious things deserve to travel, to be found, and to be honoured at the table they arrive at.

Cooking

How to use saffron:
five techniques every cook should know

The most common mistake with saffron is using it incorrectly. Added dry to a hot pan, the compounds that give saffron its colour and flavour volatilise before they can dissolve into the dish. The result is inferior colour, muted aroma, and wasted money. Here is how to use saffron the right way.

1. Blooming in warm liquid

The most reliable technique: crush a pinch of saffron threads between your fingers, then steep them in 3–4 tablespoons of warm (not boiling) water, stock, or milk for at least 15 minutes. The liquid turns a deep orange-gold. Add this liquid — threads and all — to your dish. The warm liquid draws out colour and aroma without the heat destroying the delicate compounds responsible for both.

2. Dry toasting before steeping

For a more intense, slightly smoky flavour, briefly toast the threads in a dry pan over very low heat for 20-30 seconds before steeping. The threads become brittle and crumble more easily, releasing their compounds faster. This technique is traditional in Persian rice dishes.

3. Grinding to a powder

Toasted threads can be ground to a fine powder using a mortar and pestle. A small pinch of powder dissolved in liquid dissolves faster and colours more evenly than threads. Useful for pastry, ice cream, and other preparations where texture matters.

4. Cold steeping overnight

For cold preparations — cocktails, cold soups, ice cream bases — steep threads in cold liquid overnight. Cold steeping extracts a slightly different flavour profile: brighter, more floral, less earthy than warm steeping.

5. How much to use

A little goes a long way. For a risotto serving four, 0.3-0.4g (a generous pinch of about 30-40 threads) is sufficient. For a larger paella serving eight, use 0.5-0.8g. More is not better — an excess of saffron turns a dish medicinal and bitter. Abrexom saffron is high-grade, so start at the lower end of any recipe's recommendation.
